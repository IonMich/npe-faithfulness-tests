# Neural Posterior Estimation Foundations

## Problem Setup

Simulation-based inference assumes a prior and a simulator:

```text
theta ~ p(theta)
x     ~ p(x | theta)
```

The likelihood `p(x | theta)` may be unavailable as a density, but sampling from
the simulator is available. NPE trains a conditional density estimator
`q_phi(theta | x)` to approximate the posterior for observations generated by
the model.

For prior-amortized NPE, the training objective is:

```text
L(phi) = E_{p(theta, x)}[-log q_phi(theta | x)]
```

Since `p(theta, x) = p(x) p(theta | x)`, this is:

```text
L(phi) = E_{p(x)}[
  KL(p(theta | x) || q_phi(theta | x))
] + constant
```

At the population optimum, `q_phi(theta | x) = p(theta | x)` for observations
inside the prior-predictive distribution. This is the main theoretical reason
NPE is a valid posterior-learning strategy.

## Why NPE Is Attractive

NPE estimates the posterior directly. After training, samples are drawn from the
learned density estimator without MCMC. This makes it useful when:

- many observations will be analyzed under the same simulator;
- the likelihood is unavailable or expensive;
- posterior samples must be produced quickly at inference time;
- the observation is high-dimensional and can be handled with a learned
  embedding or summary network.

The tradeoff is that simulation and training cost move upfront. For one isolated
observation and a cheap analytic likelihood, exact MCMC/HMC may be simpler and
more reliable.

## Amortized NPE Versus Sequential NPE

Amortized NPE trains on broad prior-predictive simulations and can be reused for
many observations. Sequential NPE (SNPE) focuses simulations around one observed
dataset `x_o` and is usually more simulation-efficient for that specific
observation.

Sequential methods are not simply "NPE with fewer simulations." If later rounds
sample parameters from a proposal `r(theta)` instead of the prior, the simulated
joint distribution changes:

```text
theta ~ r(theta)
x     ~ p(x | theta)
```

An uncorrected density estimator trained on this joint learns the proposal
posterior:

```text
p_r(theta | x) proportional to p(x | theta) r(theta)
```

The desired posterior remains:

```text
p(theta | x) proportional to p(x | theta) p(theta)
```

Therefore sequential NPE needs a valid proposal correction, a method such as
APT/SNPE-C, or a design such as truncated proposals. Proposal support is
critical: if `r(theta)` has little or no mass where the true posterior has mass,
later corrections cannot recover missing regions.

## Relation To NLE And NRE

NPE is one of three common neural SBI families:

- NPE learns `p(theta | x)` directly and samples quickly after training.
- NLE learns `p(x | theta)` and then samples the posterior with MCMC/VI using
  the learned likelihood.
- NRE learns a likelihood-to-evidence or likelihood-ratio surrogate and also
  requires an additional posterior sampling step.

NPE is usually the first method to try when fast posterior sampling matters.
NLE/NRE are useful cross-checks, especially when iid observations or likelihood
composition make direct posterior learning inefficient.

## Common Failure Modes

NPE can fail even when the posterior family looks expressive enough.

- Simulator misspecification: `x_o` is not plausible under the prior predictive
  distribution.
- Summary insufficiency: the context discards information needed to identify
  posterior shape or correlations.
- Proposal/support error: sequential rounds focus on the wrong region or omit a
  tail/mode.
- Conditional learning bias: the estimator can represent `p(theta | x_o)` but
  does not learn the map `x -> p(theta | x)` accurately enough from finite
  simulations.
- Symmetry or label switching: the estimator averages across equivalent modes
  incorrectly unless the parameterization or diagnostics respect the symmetry.
- Boundary and ordering constraints: flows operate best in unconstrained
  coordinates, not on hard bounded or ordered spaces.
- Overconfidence: neural posterior estimates can be too narrow even when plots
  look reasonable.

This repo has evidence for conditional learning bias: oracle density fitting on
posterior samples is much closer to the strict target than simulation-trained
NPE, while local and sequential simulation focusing improve but do not remove
the gap.

## Practical Implications

Treat NPE as an approximate inference engine until diagnostics say otherwise.
A strong workflow is:

1. Define the simulator, prior, and observed-data representation.
2. Run prior predictive checks for `x_o`.
3. Train a broad amortized baseline.
4. If broad amortization is wasteful, use sequential/local focusing with
   explicit proposal support checks.
5. Compare to an independent reference when available.
6. Run posterior predictive checks and calibration diagnostics.
7. Report failure modes, not only best runs.
